We evaluate decision-boundary proximity using DeepFool, an untargeted L2 minimal-perturbation attack applied to all correctly classified test samples from the Caltech-101 dataset. All three architectures --- VGG19, ResNet50, and DenseNet121 --- achieve a 100\% fooling rate, confirming that DeepFool reliably crosses every decision boundary when allowed sufficient iterations (max\_iter=50). The per-sample L2 distances therefore serve as a proxy for each model's classification margin: larger distances indicate a wider decision boundary margin and greater intrinsic robustness.

Among the three architectures, VGG19 exhibits the largest average decision-boundary distance (mean L2 = 1.0412, 95\% CI [0.9959, 1.0866]), indicating that its learned representations maintain the widest margin between class regions. In contrast, DenseNet121 shows the smallest average perturbation distance (mean L2 = 0.3486, std = 0.2245), suggesting that its decision boundaries lie closer to the data manifold and are therefore more susceptible to adversarial manipulation. A Mann-Whitney U test confirms that the L2 distributions differ significantly across all model pairs (p < 0.001).

The average number of DeepFool iterations provides insight into the local curvature of the decision boundary. DenseNet121 requires the most iterations on average (5.30 steps), despite having the smallest perturbation magnitude, suggesting a highly curved (non-linear) boundary geometry. Conversely, VGG19 reaches the boundary in fewer steps (3.54 iterations) but with larger perturbations, consistent with flatter, more linear decision surfaces. These findings indicate a trade-off between margin width and boundary complexity, with implications for adversarial defence design.
