We evaluate the adversarial robustness of three CNN architectures—VGG19, ResNet50, and DenseNet121—under Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) attack with 10 iterations and perturbation budgets $\varepsilon \in \{0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.04\}$. Starting from clean accuracies of 93.56\%, 94.82\%, and 95.86\% respectively, all models suffer catastrophic degradation: at $\varepsilon = 0.04$, VGG19 retains only 1.61\% accuracy, ResNet50 2.07\%, and DenseNet121 0.00\%. To quantify overall robustness, we compute the Robust Area Under Curve (RAUC), defined as the normalized integral of the accuracy-vs-$\varepsilon$ curve. VGG19 achieves RAUC = 0.1200, ResNet50 = 0.1096, and DenseNet121 = 0.0603, indicating that VGG19 exhibits the highest overall robustness, while DenseNet121 collapses most rapidly.

Analysis of confidence collapse reveals a striking pattern. Notably, VGG19 (mean Δconf = -0.0066), ResNet50 (mean Δconf = -0.0056), DenseNet121 (mean Δconf = -0.0352) exhibit negative confidence drop, meaning the models are \textit{more} confident on adversarial (incorrect) predictions than on clean inputs—a severe calibration failure indicating overconfident misclassification. Furthermore, by comparing FGSM (single-step) and PGD (multi-step) fooling rates, we derive a curvature indicator that reflects the local geometry of each model's decision boundary. ResNet50 exhibits the largest PGD-FGSM gap (mean Δ = +43.16 pp), suggesting a highly curved decision surface that single-step attacks fail to fully exploit. In contrast, DenseNet121 shows the smallest gap (Δ = +22.21 pp), indicating a more linear decision boundary where FGSM already approximates the worst-case perturbation direction. These findings highlight that architectural inductive biases not only determine clean accuracy but fundamentally shape the geometry of the learned decision boundaries, with direct consequences for adversarial vulnerability and the effectiveness of gradient-based attack strategies.
